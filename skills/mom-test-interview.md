# Mom Test Interview Discipline

Named after Rob Fitzpatrick's *The Mom Test*. The discipline of conducting and scripting customer interviews so that even your mother (who loves you and wants you to feel good) can't lie to you about your idea.

## Core rules

1. **Talk about their life, not your idea.** Ask about how they did X, not whether they like X.
2. **Ask about specific past behavior, not future intent.** "Last time this came up, what did you do?" not "Would you use this?"
3. **Talk less, listen more.** A good interview is the user describing their workflow with you nodding.

## What you can ask (good questions)

- "Walk me through the last time {situation} happened."
- "What did you actually do?"
- "What tools did you use? What did you switch to next?"
- "How long did it take? What was annoying?"
- "What did you do as a workaround?"
- "Did you pay for anything to make this easier? How much?"
- "Have you ever tried to solve this? What happened?"
- "Who else has this problem? What did they do?"
- "What didn't I ask that I should have?"

## What you cannot ask (bad questions)

- ❌ "Would you use {feature}?"
- ❌ "Do you think {idea} is a good idea?"
- ❌ "How important is {thing} to you, on a scale of 1–10?"
- ❌ "How often do you usually {action}?" — "usually" is hypothetical; ask about the last specific time.
- ❌ "Would you pay for this?" — fake commitment is worse than no commitment.
- ❌ "What features would you want?"
- ❌ "If we built X, would you use it?"

## What to listen for (signal)

- **Currency.** Time, money, social capital they actually spent. "I bought a $19 app." "I spent two hours on Sunday." "I asked my brother to help."
- **Workarounds.** What they did to compensate for the problem.
- **Frustrations.** Present-tense, specific complaints.
- **Past purchases of the alternative.** They bought something to solve this; the alternative is the real competition.
- **Commitments.** They introduced you to someone, paid a deposit, blocked time for a follow-up.

## What to ignore (noise)

- Compliments. ("Great idea!" — meaningless.)
- Hypothetical agreement. ("Yeah, I'd probably use that.")
- "On a scale" answers.
- Ideas. (You came for problems, not solutions.)
- Praise of you, the person.

## Process for the agent

1. **Drafting a script**: produce 6–10 past-behavior questions. No future-intent questions. Get the human to review.
2. **The human conducts.** You do not interview real users (Exception #4). The human runs the call; pastes the transcript.
3. **Synthesizing**: extract verbatim quotes. Tag each with: behavior / currency / frustration / workaround / surprise / noise. The "noise" tag captures compliments and hypotheticals — count them but don't synthesize on them.
4. **Anti-pattern check.** Read the transcript for the agent's bad-question patterns. If the human asked future-intent questions, flag for the next interview.

## Hard rules for synthesis

- A claim about user behavior must cite a verbatim line in the transcript.
- A "they would" statement is forbidden — replace with "they did" backed by a quote, or strike the claim.
- Compliments do NOT count as evidence for any claim.
- Don't synthesize a persona on fewer than 3 transcripts (User Researcher rule).

## Anti-patterns the skill prevents

- Founder-flattery interviews where everyone loves the idea.
- Synthesis built on what users said they'd do.
- Personas backed by zero evidence.
- "Validation" via biased questioning.
